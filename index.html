    • What is Paralysis?

    Paralysis is the inability to move the muscles you intend to. In the case of facial paralysis this means the muscles in your face won't move when you want them to. This can be unilateral (on one side of your face) or bilateral (on both sides). The paralysis can be complete or partial and can be temporary or permanent.


    When one side of your face is paralyzed, the other side can become overactive and pull your facial features towards the side that is not paralyzed.


    The eye is an important thing to keep in mind with facial paralysis. You might find that it is harder to blink or close your eye on the side that is paralyzed.

    This is dangerous for your eye as it can get injured from things like dirt or dryness. It can also make it hard to sleep if it is bright out. You can protect your eye by putting in eyedrops and taping it closed.
    You might notice that when you feel like you're closing your eye, even though the eyelid doesn't move, your eye rolls back. This is called Bell’s reflex and something our body developed as extra protection

    Causes

    There are many different causes for facial paralysis. It can be both congenital (you are born with it) or acquired (it happened after birth). If you weren’t born with it, there are two main reasons you are experiencing facial paralysis:

    • There is damage to the area of your brain that sends the signals to your face.
    • There is damage to your facial nerve and the signals aren’t going through.
    Paralysis happens when there is damage to the nerve or brain due to: trauma, cancer, inflammation, infectious conditions (Bell’s, Ramsay-Hunt, etc), stroke, acoustic neuromas, Lyme disease, or other reasons.
    Testing and examining your medical history can help the doctor understand why your facial paralysis symptoms are happening as well as help you figure out a path forward.

    Bell's Palsy

    Bell’s Palsy is the most common cause of facial paralysis and is not your fault. It happens suddenly, and generally leaves one side of your face paralyzed (flaccid paralysis). The cause is not known currently but it is thought to be due to viral inflammation or infection and can happen to anybody of any age.

    Most people will gradually improve and regain normal control of their facial muscles. It is expected that you will see improvement after a few weeks and complete recovery around the 12 month mark. For some people, the paralysis is permanent while others will develop synkinesis, which is tightness and involuntary spasms of the face. Others can experience Bell’s episodes more than once but this is rare.

    If you feel like you might have Bell’s Palsy, even though it normally improves, it is still important to go see a doctor as soon as you can so they can track your progress.

    Stages

    Facial paralysis can be described as:

    • Flaccid (complete) paralysis: At this stage you will have loss of movement and your face might feel floppy, you might have dry eyes, a dry mouth, altered taste, difficulty eating, speaking or drinking. This stage happens because there is damage to the facial nerve and/or the signal is not being properly sent to the face
    • Recovering facial paralysis: At this stage you may see symmetry improving with gradual increase in muscle strength. Focused muscle-strengthening exercises can help
    • Incomplete facial paralysis: despite recovery, there may be residual weakness at rest or with facial expressions.
    • Synkinesis: At this stage you might experience linked or unwanted facial movements. When you try to move one part of your face, you might see involuntary movement in other areas.

    Care Point

    Everyone has a unique journey with facial paralysis and it is important to understand that the stages outlined above are general guides but you might experience a different timeline.

    Synkinesis

    Synkinesis is a long-term condition that causes movement in muscles you did not mean to move. Synkinesis results when the facial nerve tries to heal itself (regenerate) but recovery results in “scrambled” or “mixed-up” signals as the nerve unintentionally grows into incorrect pathways.

    If you think about smiling, normally only your mouth would move. With synkinesis, your eye might close as well. This is just one example, but can happen anywhere in the face.

    Synkinesis will not go away by itself, but there are some things that you can do:

    • Botox can relax the muscle with too many signals to it
    • Physical therapy can help you retrain your brain to move the muscles you are trying to move, for example relaxing overly tight muscles while strengthening weak muscles.
    • Surgery can be done to remove specific branches of the facial nerve causing problematic facial movement (selective neurectomy).

    Recovery & Impacts

    Recovery is a journey and everyone will have a different path. There will be times that everything goes to plan and other times where it feels like you're at the bottom of a mountain. Your care team is there to support you throughout this process.

    Sometimes facial paralysis can improve and other times it is permanent. Facial paralysis can also make your face feel tight or be painful. Every step in the treatment, recovery and results is different for each person.

    Even with intervention, you may not regain the same facial symmetry that you used to have. Many people have shared that they feel self-conscious, or want to isolate themselves after developing facial paralysis. It is important to ask for support and help through this time.

    It is not vain or self-centered to mourn the loss of your face and to want your old face back. It is important to recognize that that may not be possible, but your care team will do what they can to help you.
