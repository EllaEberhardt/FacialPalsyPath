    This can be unilateral (on one side of your face) or bilateral (on both sides). Paralysis can also be complete or partial and can be temporary or permanent.


    What happens to the unparalyzed side of your face?

    When one side of your face is paralyzed, the other side can become overactive and pull your facial features towards the side that is not paralyzed.


    What happens to your eye on the paralysed side?

    The eye is an important thing to keep in mind with facial paralysis. You might find that it is harder to blink or close your eye on the side that is paralyzed. This is normal, as the facial nerve is responsible for helping close your eyelid.

    A different nerve, the oculomotor nerve (cranial nerve III), is responsible for opening your eyelid. You will be able to open your eye even with facial paralysis. You can protect your eye by putting in eyedrops and taping it closed.



    Protect your eye from:

    Dirt

    Wind

    Dryness


    Why does my eye roll back when I try to close it?


    You might notice that when you feel like you're closing your eye, even though the eyelid doesn't move, your eye rolls back.

    This is called Bell’s reflex and something our body developed as extra protection.

    Facial Nerve

    There are many different causes for facial paralysis. It can be either congenital (you are born with it) or acquired (it happened after birth). If you weren’t born with it, there are two main reasons you are experiencing facial paralysis:

    • There is damage to the area of your brain that sends the signals to your face.
    • There is damage to your facial nerve and the signals aren’t going through.
    Paralysis happens when there is damage to the nerve or brain due to:


    Trauma

    Cancer

    Inflammation

    Infectious Conditions (Bell’s, Ramsay-Hunt, etc)

    Stroke

    Acoustic Neuroma

    Lyme Disease

    More


    Testing and examining your medical history can help the doctor understand why your facial paralysis symptoms are happening as well as help you figure out a path forward.

    Facial Muscles

    The facial muscles are responsible for the movements of the face, including smiling, frowning, and closing the eyes. These muscles are innervated by the facial nerve (cranial nerve VII).

    Facial Nerve

    The facial nerve (cranial nerve VII) is responsible for controlling the muscles of facial expression and is also involved in taste sensation and salivation.
