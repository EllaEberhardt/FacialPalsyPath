    • Intro

    This page acts as a hub for different ways that we can treat facial paralysis. The decision on what best suits your case is up to your care team. They are specialized to think of every option and find the best fit for you.
    They will consider things like:

    • Your type of paralysis
    • The duration of your paralysis
    • Your nerve’s health
    • Your age
    • What your desired outcomes are
    • The invasiveness of each surgery

    Surgical treatment can either be static, they restore your resting facial symmetry, or dynamic, work to give you back movement of your facial muscles.

    Non-Surgical Options

    There are several non-surgical treatments available for facial paralysis, including:


    Botox

    Botox (botulinum toxin) is an injectable that can be used to treat facial paralysis patients with synkinesis. It works to relax the muscles it is injected into on your face or neck. It can help:

    • Improve resting symmetry
    • Relieve tight muscles
    • Improve control over facial muscles

    Physiotherapy

    Facial therapy can help you relearn how to move the muscles in your face after a facial paralysis diagnosis. A physiotherapist will work together with you to help improve facial movement and coordination. Physiotherapy can be extremely helpful under the guidance of a specialized facial therapist.

    Speech Therapy

    Speech therapy can help you regain control of your mouth muscles and a speech therapist can work with you to improve speech, strengthen facial muscles and retrain oral coordination.

    Surgical Options: Static

    Static Procedures

    Static procedures are ones that help your face have more natural resting symmetry. This means when you are not moving your face, it will look more symmetrical to everyone around you

    Eyelid surgery

    With facial paralysis, you lose control of your upper eyelid making it difficult to blink or close your eye. This is dangerous as you lose the ability to protect it from getting scratched by things like dirt, or dryness. During this procedure, a small and thin eyelid weight will be put under the skin of your upper eyelid that will help weight it down to close. The muscle to open your eye is a different nerve (not the facial nerve), so your ability to open your eye will be the same as before paralysis. If you regain function or anything changes, the weight can be later removed or changed. You can safely go through a metal detector or MRI with the weight. Your doctor may tape trial weights to your eyelid during the clinic visit to test which weight works best for you.

    Static Facial Suspension (Sling)

    Fascia Lata Facial Suspension - A band of fascia lata is taken from your body (generally from your wrist or leg) and used like a sling to hold up the corner of the mouth, upper lip, laugh line or base of the nostril.

    Muscle Excision

    During this procedure the surgeon will remove the muscle that has too much activity and has become too strong. When the muscle is too strong it can pull the face to one side or the other and feel tight. The muscle that is too strong is the one on the unparalyzed side of your face. DAO Excision - The depressor anguli oris muscle attaches the corner of the mouth to the lower jaw and can pull the side of the mouth down if it is too strong. Its removal can permanently improve your smile symmetry.

    Selective Neurectomy

    During this procedure nerve branches that are causing synkinesis or causing some muscles to work too strongly will be removed.

    Surgical Options: Dynamic

    Dynamic Procedures

    Dynamic procedures are ones that help you regain control of the movement in your facial muscles. Everyone will have different results and recovery takes time. Retraining your brain to connect with your muscles is not an easy task.

    Nerve Transfer

    Using the nerve to one of the chewing muscles (masseter) and rewiring it to one of the facial nerve branches that controls smile, sometimes known as masseter nerve transfer.

    Nerve Graft

    A nerve graft is done by taking a nerve from elsewhere in your body (generally the sural nerve in your leg) and stitching it to the healthy facial nerve on the non-paralyzed side of your face. The nerve is then passed across your face and connected to the branches on the paralyzed side or saved for later use (ex: gracilis free flap surgery).

    Free Muscle Transfer

    During this procedure a muscle is taken from somewhere in your body and placed into your face to help you regain movement. This is generally done after upwards of 2 years of facial paralysis after there is no sign of recovery. Gracilis Free Flap - A small piece of the gracilis muscles (inner thigh) can be transferred to the face to help recreate your smile. This muscle needs to be connected to blood vessels to stay healthy and the nerve to move. This muscle can be connected to the healthy facial nerve on the non-paralyzed side of your face using a cross-face nerve graft, or connected to a branch of the masseteric nerve (one for chewing). The cross-face nerve graft has a higher chance of letting you smile spontaneously but the masseteric nerve connection is more reliable. With the masseteric nerve connection, you will likely have to think about biting down in order to smile.
